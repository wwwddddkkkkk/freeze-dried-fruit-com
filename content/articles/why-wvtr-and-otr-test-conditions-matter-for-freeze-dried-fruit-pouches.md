Barrier numbers are useful only when the buyer knows what question the lab was actually asked.

That is easy to forget in freeze-dried fruit packaging because supplier spec sheets often compress the context into one short line: `WVTR` or `OTR`, one number, one unit, done. The number feels objective. The comparison often is not.

## The direct answer

WVTR and OTR test conditions matter because barrier performance is reported under defined laboratory conditions, not as a single universal truth. If two freeze-dried fruit pouch specs do not match on method, temperature, humidity, and units, the numbers can mislead even when both are technically correct.

For buyers, that means a barrier comparison should start with the test conditions before it starts with the ranking.

## Why the same pouch number can move

Barrier materials do not behave identically across every environment. Test standards exist because permeability has to be measured in a controlled way.

In practice, that means the reported value depends on details such as:

- the test method used
- the test temperature
- the relative humidity used for the test
- whether the reading applies to film or a finished package
- the thickness and structure being tested

For freeze-dried fruit, this matters because the commercial question is never just, "Is this pouch a barrier?" The real question is whether the package keeps a dry, porous product crisp long enough in the storage and opening pattern the channel will impose.

## WVTR first, then OTR

When buyers are new to the category, oxygen often sounds like the more sophisticated packaging conversation. For freeze-dried fruit, water vapor is usually the faster problem.

If water vapor protection is weak, the customer tends to feel it quickly:

- crunch softens
- thin pieces go leathery
- powder cakes
- fruit clumps
- the pouch starts tasting tired even before it looks damaged

That is why WVTR usually gets the first read. OTR still matters, especially for color-sensitive berry products or oxidation-sensitive blends, but humidity is commonly the first route to disappointment.

::: note "Useful comparison rule"
If two pouch specs do not show the same barrier test context, treat the numbers as separate data points, not as a clean ranking.
:::

## Why mismatched conditions create bad buying decisions

Imagine two suppliers offering similar resealable pouches for freeze-dried strawberry.

One reports a very low WVTR, but the conditions are not shown clearly. Another reports a somewhat higher number but gives the method, the temperature, the relative humidity, the units, and the full laminate structure.

The second supplier may actually be giving the more usable answer.

The buying error is assuming the smaller number automatically means the stronger commercial pouch. Without matched conditions, the comparison may be no better than comparing two moisture readings taken by different methods and pretending they are identical.

That is especially risky when buyers compare:

- clear versus metallized structures
- single-serve versus family packs
- dry-climate distribution versus humid retail channels
- one-time-use packs versus pouches opened repeatedly over weeks

Those are not the same packaging job, even if the fruit inside is similar.

## The spec-sheet questions that make barrier data usable

When reviewing pouch options for freeze-dried fruit, ask for the barrier number and the frame around it:

1. What test method was used?
2. At what temperature was it run?
3. What relative humidity condition applied?
4. What units are being reported?
5. Is the result for film alone or the finished package?
6. What is the actual laminate structure and nominal thickness?

Those questions turn a packaging claim into something that can be audited and compared.

They also make it easier to connect the lab result back to the product reality. A large resealable mango pouch with repeated kitchen exposure is a different risk profile from a small single-serve apple pack sold for immediate use.

## Barrier numbers still do not replace the full packaging read

Even perfectly matched test conditions do not finish the job.

Freeze-dried fruit buyers still need to understand:

- seal integrity
- zipper performance
- headspace volume
- fill weight and pack geometry
- whether the product carries a desiccant or absorber
- whether the shelf-life work included texture, not only safety

A pouch can post respectable barrier data and still underperform because the seal is weak or the opening pattern is unrealistic for the barrier build.

## Bottom line

WVTR and OTR numbers are worth asking for, but they are worth trusting only when the test conditions come with them. In freeze-dried fruit packaging, the buyer who compares barrier data without matching the context is often comparing paperwork quality, not pouch quality.

Start with the conditions. Then decide whether the pouch is actually built for the fruit, the channel, and the opening pattern you expect.
