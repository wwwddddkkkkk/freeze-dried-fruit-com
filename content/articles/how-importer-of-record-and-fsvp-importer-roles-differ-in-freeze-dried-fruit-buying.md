In imported freeze-dried fruit, the first paperwork confusion is often also the most expensive one.

Teams say "the importer" as if it were one person.

It often is not.

## The direct answer

The importer of record and the FSVP importer differ because they answer different questions.

The importer of record is the customs-entry role. The FSVP importer is the food-safety verification role under FDA's Foreign Supplier Verification Programs framework.

Those roles can sit with the same entity. FDA is explicit that they do not have to.

For buyers, the practical implication is simple: if imported freeze-dried fruit is moving into the United States, somebody must be clearly identified for:

- entry and customs-side execution
- FSVP ownership
- prior-notice and filer coordination
- records and FDA response

When those responsibilities are blurred, the shipment may still move. The problem appears later, when a hold, document request, or supplier deviation has to be answered quickly.

## What the importer of record does

The importer of record is tied to the customs side of the transaction.

That role is the legal entry control point for CBP purposes, typically working directly or through a licensed customs broker. In a freeze-dried fruit transaction, that usually means the importer-of-record side is closest to:

- entry filing
- importer identification
- duty and fee handling
- customs documentation flow
- broker coordination

That does **not** automatically mean the same party understands the food-safety program behind the fruit.

FDA's FSMA FAQ makes the distinction directly: the importer of record may be the same as the FSVP importer, but it is not necessarily. FDA also notes that the CBP importer of record is not required by CBP to verify that the imported food was produced under applicable U.S. food-safety requirements.

That is the key line many commercial teams miss.

## What the FSVP importer does

The FSVP importer is the food-safety owner under FDA's imported-food framework.

FDA defines that importer as the U.S. owner or consignee of the food at the time of entry, or, if there is no such U.S. owner or consignee, a designated U.S. agent or representative of the foreign owner or consignee who has signed to serve in that role.

That role owns the supplier-verification side of the import program, including the logic behind questions such as:

- Has the foreign supplier been properly evaluated?
- Is there an adequate hazard analysis?
- What verification activity supports ongoing approval?
- Are the required records available if FDA asks?

In other words, the FSVP importer is not mainly a freight role or a broker role. It is the party that must be able to show why importing the food is appropriately controlled.

## Why freeze-dried fruit buyers get this wrong

Imported freeze-dried fruit commonly moves through layered supply structures:

- foreign manufacturer
- trader or export house
- U.S. importer or distributor
- private-label brand
- customs broker
- warehouse or repacker

That structure makes the word *importer* dangerously vague.

A brand may think the distributor is handling everything. A distributor may assume the named importer is the FSVP party. A broker may correctly file the entry but have no role in supplier verification. The foreign supplier may believe its U.S. agent is only a communication contact, not the FSVP importer.

Each assumption can sound reasonable until FDA asks for records or a shipment is detained.

## Where the handoffs usually fail

The biggest failure points are usually not dramatic legal misunderstandings. They are ordinary operational gaps.

Examples:

- nobody confirmed who is listed as the FSVP importer for each line of the entry
- the commercial buyer assumed the importer of record was also keeping supplier-verification records
- the FSVP importer was named, but the broker or filer did not receive the correct identifying information
- the supplier-approval file existed, but not in a form retrievable fast enough for an FDA request

FDA's FSVP guidance is useful here because it explains that the FSVP importer is responsible for ensuring the identifying information is provided through the entry process, even if another party physically submits the data.

That means "our broker handles it" is not a complete answer.

## What to lock down before the first purchase order

Before placing the first order for imported freeze-dried fruit, a buyer should know five things in writing:

1. Who is the importer of record?
2. Who is the FSVP importer?
3. Who is providing the FSVP importer identification to the filer or broker?
4. Who holds the supplier-verification records and can produce them quickly?
5. Who answers FDA if the agency questions the shipment or requests records?

If those answers point to different parties, that is not automatically a problem.

The problem is leaving the split implicit.

## Why prior notice adds another layer of confusion

Prior notice makes the chain look even more unified than it really is, because many kinds of parties can participate in that step. FDA's prior-notice guidance says that manufacturers, exporters, brokers, importers, U.S. agents, and others with the required information may submit it.

That flexibility is operationally useful.

It also means a team can mistake whoever handled prior notice for the party actually carrying the broader compliance burden.

Those are not the same thing.

## The smartest buyer habit

The best habit is to stop asking, "Who is the importer?" and start asking:

- Who is the customs-entry party?
- Who is the FSVP party?
- Who will receive and answer FDA questions?
- Who can actually retrieve the records inside one business day?

Those questions sound more cumbersome, but they are much closer to the real operating model.

## Bottom line

In freeze-dried fruit buying, the importer of record and the FSVP importer can be the same entity, but they do different jobs. One is the customs-entry control point. The other is the food-safety verification control point.

If the roles are split, the split should be explicit before the shipment moves. That clarity is not paperwork theater. It is what keeps an imported-food program from breaking under the first real test.
