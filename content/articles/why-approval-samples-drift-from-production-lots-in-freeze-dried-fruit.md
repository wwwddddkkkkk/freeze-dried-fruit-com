Approval samples are supposed to reduce risk.

In freeze-dried fruit, they often create a false sense of certainty instead. The sample tastes bright, the pieces look intact, the pouch seems acceptable, and the team quietly assumes the later shipment will mean the same thing.

That assumption is where the drift starts.

## The direct answer

Approval samples drift from production lots when the sample is approved as a visual and sensory impression rather than as a fully defined commercial standard. In freeze-dried fruit, lot-to-lot changes in raw material, cut geometry, breakage, moisture behavior, packaging, and transit handling can all widen the gap unless the buyer tests repeatability against a written specification.

One good sample can show possibility. It does not prove routine execution.

## The sample is usually cleaner than the system

A sample often travels through a narrower and more favorable path than a routine shipment.

For example, the sample may be:

- selected from the strongest pieces in the run
- packed in a small pouch rather than the commercial case format
- shipped quickly with minimal handling
- reviewed before long storage exposure
- judged without full documentation or transit history

None of that is automatically deceptive. It simply means the sample can reflect capability more than normal output.

The practical error is treating that capability snapshot as if it already equals the daily commercial lot.

## Why the written specification matters more than memory

The moment a buyer says "match the approved sample," the buyer has created room for argument unless the sample's important traits are already written down.

The useful commercial definition should cover more than taste:

- fruit identity and format
- piece size or cut window
- broken-piece tolerance
- target moisture or water activity
- color and aroma expectations
- packaging structure
- coding and release documents

USDA's freeze-dried-fruit description is helpful here because it reinforces the discipline of separating product characteristics, defects, and acceptance language. That is exactly what keeps the sample from becoming a vague reference object.

## Raw fruit variability does not disappear because the sample was good

Freeze-dried fruit inherits variability from the fruit itself. Even when the process is controlled, the supplier is still working with agricultural raw material that shifts by:

- harvest window
- maturity
- soluble solids
- size and trim yield
- seed or peel burden
- color intensity

That means the approval sample may represent one strong intake condition while the later shipment reflects a more ordinary one. If the buyer did not define what level of shift is still acceptable, the lot arrives as a surprise instead of as a controlled comparison.

This is one reason premium programs review more than one lot before calling the product truly approved.

## Packaging and shipping create a second layer of drift

A freeze-dried fruit sample can look excellent before the real packaging system and real transport stress begin doing their work.

Common drift points include:

- more powder at the bottom of the bag
- softer texture after longer storage or humid transit
- different headspace or fill density
- different pouch barrier than the sample format implied
- carton or pallet handling that increases breakage

That is why a commercial approval should include the actual pack system whenever possible. Fruit approval without packaging approval is only partial approval.

## Documents are part of the product

Many teams isolate the sensory sample from the release packet. That is a mistake.

If the program depends on supplier consistency, the commercial lot should arrive with documents that confirm the same product logic every time:

- specification sheet
- certificate of analysis
- lot coding and traceability
- certificate scope where relevant
- packaging details where relevant

The sample may win trust. The paperwork is what lets the trust scale.

FDA's supplier-evaluation framework is useful here because it centers documented performance and approval logic, not only first impressions.

## What stronger approval looks like

A better freeze-dried-fruit approval process does four things:

1. It locks the SKU definition before the relationship grows.
2. It compares more than one lot.
3. It includes the real package and transit path.
4. It defines what kind of drift triggers rejection or requalification.

Those habits make the eventual disagreement narrower and more factual. Instead of arguing whether the lot "feels different," the buyer can point to specific drift in breakage, size, moisture behavior, or packaging performance.

## Bottom line

Approval samples drift from production lots when the sample stands in for a system that was never fully defined. Freeze-dried fruit is too variable, fragile, and packaging-dependent for that shortcut.

Approve the commercial meaning of the SKU, not only the beauty of the first bag. That is the difference between a promising sample and a repeatable supply program.
