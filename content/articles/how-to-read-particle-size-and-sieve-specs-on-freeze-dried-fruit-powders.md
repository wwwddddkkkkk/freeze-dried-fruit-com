Powder specifications often look more precise than they really are.

The line may say 80 mesh, under 300 microns, or 95 percent through a stated sieve. That feels exact. But unless you understand what the line is controlling, how it was measured, and what the powder is supposed to do next, the number can still be easy to misread.

## The direct answer

Read freeze-dried fruit powder particle-size specs in five layers: the unit language, the control objective, the measurement method, the application fit, and the normal production range. A number is only useful when you know whether it is protecting against grit, dust, clumping, slow hydration, or some other real use-case problem.

The practical error is to treat "fine powder" as if it were self-explanatory.

## 1. Translate the language before you judge the powder

Start by decoding what the spec is actually saying.

Common expressions include:

- U.S. Standard sieve number
- mesh
- microns
- percent through
- percent retained
- top-size limit

These do not all describe the same thing in the same way. A sieve number describes a screen classification. A micron value describes a dimensional scale. A "percent through" line tells you how much material passes a stated opening. A "percent retained" line tells you how much stays behind.

USDA's freeze-dried fruit commodity language is useful here because it shows the logic clearly: size expectations become measurable when the spec states how much of a product may pass through a specific sieve. That same logic shows up in powder specifications even when the format is finer and the application is more technical.

## 2. Ask what the spec is trying to control

Not every particle-size line is solving the same commercial problem.

A powder spec may be trying to control:

- coarse particles that create grit
- excess fines that increase dusting
- flow behavior
- hydration speed
- visual texture in a topping or coating

That distinction matters because one cut point can hide the wrong risk. A powder can comply with a maximum top size and still carry too many fines. It can also comply with a fine average while still containing enough coarse fragments to feel sandy in a drink or filling.

The smart question is not only "how fine is it?" but "what failure is this line meant to prevent?"

## 3. Method matters more than the pretty number

NIST's particle-size guidance makes an important point: size-distribution results depend on the method used to measure them.

That matters a lot for freeze-dried fruit powders because the particles are irregular. They are not ideal spheres. They can fracture, agglomerate, hold static, or contain mixed shapes from fiber, skin, and pulp structure. A sieve analysis and an instrument method such as laser-based analysis do not always describe that same powder in identical terms.

For buyers, that means a usable spec should answer:

- what method was used?
- how was the sample prepared?
- was the powder dispersed or simply screened as received?
- was the result based on one test or routine production data?

Without that context, two suppliers can hand over particle-size numbers that look comparable but were produced through different logic.

## 4. Match the distribution to the job

The right powder is the one that behaves properly in the finished application.

Finer distributions often work better when the powder needs:

- rapid hydration
- smooth mouthfeel
- even color spread
- low visible particulate in a glaze, filling, or drink

Coarser or more controlled distributions may work better when the powder needs:

- visible fruit identity
- reduced dusting
- slower release in a topping
- more texture in a bakery or confectionery system

That is why asking for the finest possible powder can be a mistake. Finer is not automatically better. In many freeze-dried fruit systems, finer also means more surface area, more dust, more caking risk, and faster flavor release than the application wanted.

## 5. Ask for the normal distribution, not only a single cut point

One compliant sample is not the same thing as a stable powder program.

Ask suppliers for the normal production view:

- what is the usual range?
- what portion of the lot typically sits near the fine end?
- what portion tends to remain coarse?
- how much lot-to-lot movement is considered normal?

This matters because powder performance is rarely decided by one particle alone. It is decided by the distribution. A powder with a broad spread may behave unpredictably in blending or in the finished consumer use even if one sample technically passes the stated cut point.

## A buyer's reading order

If the powder spec is short, read it in this order:

1. What unit language is being used?
2. Is the line controlling coarse particles, fines, or both?
3. What method generated the result?
4. Does that distribution fit the intended application?
5. What does normal production look like outside the single sample?

That sequence turns the size line from background noise into a real buying tool.

## Conclusion

Particle-size and sieve specs on freeze-dried fruit powders are not lab decoration. They are shorthand for how the powder will behave when it is poured, blended, hydrated, dusted, coated, or eaten.

The best reading habit is to stop asking whether the powder sounds fine and start asking what the distribution is protecting. That is where the useful answer usually begins.
